Langston Hughes wrote many strong poems. "I Look at the World" is the best one and one of my favorite. His other poem "I, Too" was also good, but this one just has that strong feeling to it. It's short but very heavy, and every time you read it, you notice something new. It basically kind of stays in your head even when you're done reading it. It's like he's not yelling at you, just calmly showing what the world feels like from his side.

Waking Up

The poem starts with Langston Hughes opening his eyes to reality:

I look at the world
From awakening eyes in a Black face
And this is what I see:
This fenced-off narrow space
Assigned to me

The fence is just a symbol of the unfair limits set by society. It's like he's waking up and realizing what's really around him. The space feels small, like he's not allowed to go past it. You can tell he didn't build that fence, and that someone else did. And it's been there so long you might just almost stop noticing it, but now he sees it clear. that's what makes this part hit deep, because once you see the fence, you can't unsee it.

Seeing the Problem

I look then at the silly walls
Through dark eyes in a dark face
And this is what I know:
That all these walls oppression builds
Will have to go!

He now calls the walls "silly." They are not natural at all, someone made them on purpose. Humans were the ones who built it and that means they can break it too. It's not some mountain or storm it's man-made object so it doesn't have to stay there forever.


It also hits you deep even more when you think about Black women too, facing the same fence plus other walls built on gender. So the poem's fight is not only set on one group, its set on a wider group. It links race, class, and gender in the same tight space.

Break those walls
Oh yeah!

Using Our Hands

I look at my own body
With eyes no longer blind
And I see that my own hands can make
The world that's in my mind
Then let us hurry, comrades
The road to find

He sees his own power and he invites his friends to help build a better world. It's like he woke up after a very long nap, and now he knows what to do. His hands are not just there for decoration, they can shape stuff and fix what's broken. He not trying to do it alone either he wants everyone to move with him.

Why It Still Matters

Four Clear Steps

  1. Look at the unfair rules.
  2. Remember that it was humans who made it.
  3. Use your own hands & power to change it.
  4. Work with comrades.

These steps help people stand up for what is right. You can see them in stuff like Black Lives Matter or when people fight for clean water. This poem might be old but the message still works now. unfair things still happen and people still have to fight them it doesn't just go away by itself.

Final Thoughts

I Look at the World is not just a poem about complaining. It's a plan. See the fence, say what it is, then build something fair and better with your comrades. This poem still matters even now because the world still has a lot of problems, and unfairness. You don't need big words or long speeches just open your eyes and use your hands. And if more people thought like this maybe stuff would change faster, and it wouldn't have to stay like this forever.
